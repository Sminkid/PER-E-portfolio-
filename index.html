Reflection on Internship Experience

Internship Learning and Professional Growth

01

What were your expectations about your internship before you joined?

Before starting my internship at Optik Consultancy, I anticipated a significant reality check. Having spent most of my degree focused on software engineering coursework, I was uncertain whether I could adapt to a professional environment, let alone lead a team effectively. I expected to occupy a supporting role, filling gaps in my knowledge quietly while more experienced colleagues took charge. Despite these reservations, I committed to approaching every challenge with a genuine willingness to learn and a determination to contribute meaningfully from day one.

02

What was the reality? How was it different from your expectations?

The reality proved far more demanding and far more rewarding than I had anticipated. Rather than easing into the team as a junior contributor, I was appointed Team Lead of a cross-disciplinary group of five engineers tasked with developing a biomedical hardware prototype from scratch. This was entirely outside my software background. I was simultaneously managing team dynamics, coordinating task allocation, navigating hardware resource constraints, and presenting progress updates directly to stakeholders. The pace of the environment demanded rapid decision-making, technical adaptability, and a level of communication clarity I had not previously been tested on. What struck me most was that leadership in practice looked nothing like leadership in theory, as it required constant recalibration based on the needs of the team and the realities of the project.

03

What lessons were the most important from your internship? Why were they important?

The most important lessons I took from this experience were resilience, adaptability, and the practical value of cross-disciplinary collaboration. Early in the project, we encountered a critical failure in our machine learning validation pipeline, a problem that threatened both our timeline and the integrity of our deliverable. Rather than concealing the issue, I escalated it immediately, coordinated a focused debugging effort across the team, and we resolved it before the client deadline. This taught me that professional accountability is not simply about avoiding mistakes. It is about how transparently and decisively you respond when things go wrong. Leading a team through that pressure also reinforced how essential open communication and mutual trust are to delivering results in any engineering context.

04

After your workplace experience, what would you say your value proposition would be to an employer? How can you demonstrate this?

I believe my value proposition to an employer lies in my ability to lead under pressure, communicate across disciplines, and deliver outcomes in unfamiliar technical territory. I can demonstrate this through the tangible results of my internship: guiding a team of five to successfully build and present a functional biomedical hardware prototype within a constrained timeline, while maintaining stakeholder alignment throughout. These are outcomes I can speak to specifically and back up with evidence.

05

How did your internship influence the type of role in which you are interested?

My internship meaningfully clarified my career direction. I am drawn to roles that sit at the intersection of technical problem-solving and human collaboration, specifically systems engineering or engineering consulting, where I can apply both my software expertise and my demonstrated capacity to lead cross-functional teams. The experience of working beyond my own discipline gave me confidence that I can add value in environments that are complex, fast-moving, and require engineers who are as comfortable communicating with clients as they are solving technical problems.
