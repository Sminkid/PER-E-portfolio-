Reflective Questions

Task 1a — Professional Identity & Work Experience

01

Describe your engineering work experience. What tasks did you perform? What projects were you involved in?

My primary engineering work experience was a six-month software engineering internship at Atlassian, a globally recognised technology company headquartered in Sydney. During this placement, I was embedded within a backend engineering team responsible for the Jira Service Management platform. My day-to-day responsibilities encompassed developing and maintaining microservices built on Java and Spring Boot, writing unit and integration tests using JUnit 5, and collaborating on the design of REST API endpoints that improved data retrieval performance. I was also involved in a larger project to refactor a legacy notification service, which required me to understand and apply SOLID design principles within a complex, distributed system. This experience provided me with meaningful exposure to enterprise-scale software development practices, including code review processes, continuous integration pipelines, and cross-functional collaboration with product managers, designers, and quality assurance engineers.

02

What technical skills did you develop or apply during your experience?

The internship significantly deepened my proficiency in Java, particularly in the context of building production-grade Spring Boot applications. I gained practical experience with RESTful API design, dependency injection, and exception handling patterns that I had previously only encountered in academic settings. Additionally, I was introduced to the team's CI/CD pipeline, which leveraged Bitbucket Pipelines and Docker, providing me with hands-on exposure to containerisation and automated deployment practices. My SQL skills were reinforced through the optimisation of database queries for the notification service, and I broadened my understanding of PostgreSQL's performance tuning capabilities. Furthermore, I developed greater competency in version control workflows, including branch strategy, pull request etiquette, and merge conflict resolution within a large collaborative repository. These technical competencies have directly complemented and contextualised the theoretical foundations established through my UTS coursework.

03

How did your work experience shape your professional identity as an engineer?

Prior to commencing my internship, my identity as an engineer was largely defined by academic performance and personal projects. However, the professional environment at Atlassian fundamentally shifted my understanding of what it means to be an engineer in practice. I came to appreciate that engineering is as much a social and communicative endeavour as it is a technical one. Participating in Agile ceremonies—sprint planning, daily stand-ups, and retrospectives—revealed the importance of clear articulation of blockers, progress, and technical trade-offs to non-technical stakeholders. Navigating code reviews taught me to approach feedback constructively and to advocate for design decisions with evidence and reasoning rather than personal preference. I now identify as an engineer who values clarity, collaboration, and continuous improvement, and who understands that software is built by people for people. This shift in perspective has made me more intentional about how I communicate and conduct myself in team settings.

04

What challenges did you face and how did you overcome them?

One of the most significant challenges I encountered during my internship was the steep learning curve associated with inheriting a large, underdocumented legacy codebase. In an academic environment, codebases are typically self-contained and well-scoped, whereas the production system I worked within spanned hundreds of thousands of lines and multiple interdependent services. To address this, I adopted a systematic approach: I spent the first two weeks mapping the architecture through documentation, architecture diagrams, and guided walkthroughs with a senior engineer who served as my mentor. I also made deliberate use of the team's internal Confluence wiki and raised questions during one-on-one meetings rather than interrupting colleagues ad hoc. A secondary challenge was managing imposter syndrome in a high-performing environment. I overcame this by setting small, achievable milestones and celebrating incremental progress, and by seeking candid feedback from my manager during fortnightly check-ins. These strategies enabled me to deliver tangible contributions within my placement period and to build confidence in my abilities.

05

How has this experience influenced your career goals?

My internship experience has crystallised a career direction that I had previously only vaguely envisioned. I am now committed to pursuing a role as a full-stack or backend software engineer within a product-focused technology company upon graduation. The experience demonstrated to me that I thrive in collaborative, problem-driven environments where I can see the direct impact of my technical contributions on end users. It has also reinforced my interest in distributed systems and cloud architecture, areas I wish to explore more deeply through the remainder of my degree and through self-directed study, including the AWS certifications I am pursuing. Longer term, I aspire to grow into a technical lead or solutions architect role, drawing on both my engineering expertise and the interpersonal skills developed through professional experience. The exposure to cross-functional teamwork at Atlassian has also demonstrated to me that the most impactful engineers are those who bridge the gap between technical complexity and human usability.
